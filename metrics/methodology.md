# Methodology & Data Definitions

## Conceptual Framework

This bibliography operationalizes the research question by:

1. **Defining "high peacebuilding investment"** as regions/countries receiving substantial, sustained flows from:
   - UN Peacebuilding Fund (PBF)
   - World Bank State & Peacebuilding Fund (SPF)
   - OECD DAC conflict-prevention ODA
   - Regional bodies (EU Instrument for Pre-Accession, African Peace Facility)

2. **Defining "low peacebuilding investment"** as regions where formal peacebuilding ODA is minimal, absent, or overwhelmed by active conflict dynamics.

3. **Measuring outcomes** via:
   - Annual conflict-related fatalities (SIPRI, UCDP, ACLED)
   - Global Peace Index (GPI) scores (IEP)
   - Fatality rates per 100,000 population

---

## Key Variable Definitions

### Conflict-Related Fatalities
- **SIPRI definition:** Deaths directly caused by armed conflict, including combatants and civilians killed in battle or targeted attacks.
- **IEP/GPI definition:** Broader — includes deaths from internal conflict, terrorism, and political violence.
- **UCDP definition:** Requires at least 25 battle deaths/year to qualify as armed conflict; fatality counts are best-estimate point values.
- **ACLED definition:** Event-based — records fatalities per incident across protest, riot, battle, explosion, and violence-against-civilians categories.

> ⚠️ **Caveat:** Fatality figures vary across sources due to differing methodologies. SIPRI 2023 global estimate = 170,700; IEP GPI 2023 = 238,000. Metrics in this bibliography use SIPRI as the primary source unless stated otherwise.

### Global Peace Index (GPI) Score
- Scale: **1.000** (most peaceful) to **5.000** (least peaceful)
- 23 indicators across 3 domains:
  1. Societal Safety & Security
  2. Ongoing Domestic & International Conflict
  3. Militarisation
- Covers 163 countries (99.7% of world population)
- Published annually by the Institute for Economics & Peace (IEP)

### Peacebuilding ODA
- Tracked under OECD DAC purpose codes 15210 (Security system management), 15220 (Civilian peacebuilding), 15230 (Post-conflict peace & reconciliation)
- Distinct from humanitarian aid and general development assistance
- PBF is a dedicated UN instrument; SPF is the World Bank equivalent

## Limitations & Caveats

1. **Attribution problem:** GPI improvements cannot be causally attributed to peacebuilding investment alone. Economic development, governance, and geopolitical factors are confounding variables.

2. **Reporting bias:** Fatality data in active conflict zones (Sudan, Gaza, Myanmar) may be under-reported due to access limitations.

3. **Investment lag:** Peacebuilding investments may take 5–15 years to manifest in measurable peace outcomes (Paris Declaration principle of long-term engagement).

4. **Selection bias:** High-investment regions (e.g., Europe) were already relatively peaceful before large-scale investment, making it difficult to isolate investment effects.

5. **PBF scale:** At $202M/year across 36 countries (~$5.6M average), PBF funding is catalytic rather than transformative. It is not comparable in scale to military spending ($2,443B globally in 2023).

6. **GPI regional averages:** Regional GPI averages are unweighted means of country scores; they do not account for population size.

---

## Recommended Analytical Approaches

- **Difference-in-differences:** Compare countries before/after PBF engagement
- **Propensity score matching:** Match conflict-affected countries by baseline conditions
- **Time-series analysis:** Track GPI and fatality trends against investment inflows
- **Effective Peacebuilding Initiative dashboard:** Use their 34-source integrated dataset for multivariate analysis
